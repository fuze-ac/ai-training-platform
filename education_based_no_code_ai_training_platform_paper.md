# Concept Paper: Education-Based No-Code AI Training Platform for Specialized Organizational AI

## Executive Summary

This paper proposes an education-based no-code AI training platform that helps non-technical users understand practical AI mechanics and then apply that understanding to train, validate, and deploy specialized AI systems for real organizational use cases. The platform is designed for schools, universities, hospitals, government agencies, local organizations, and SMEs that want to adopt AI but lack AI engineers, data scientists, or mature AI governance practices.

Unlike conventional AI education platforms that mainly teach coding or AI concepts, this project combines learning and implementation. Users learn how AI works at a practical level — including data preparation, model behavior, retrieval-augmented generation, prompt logic, fine-tuning concepts, evaluation, privacy, deployment, and continuous improvement — and then use guided no-code tools to build AI systems from their own organizational data.

The project also differs from enterprise platforms such as Google Vertex AI AutoML, AWS SageMaker Canvas, Microsoft AI Builder, IBM watsonx.ai, and private/on-premise AI infrastructure platforms. Those platforms provide powerful technical capabilities, but they are largely tool-first or infrastructure-first. This proposal focuses on the missing middle layer: AI capacity-building. It aims to make organizations capable of understanding, adapting, and governing AI independently, not merely consuming AI tools.

The proposed framework is:

**Learn → Prepare → Train → Validate → Deploy → Improve**

The expected outcome is not only a trained AI model or chatbot, but a repeatable educational and operational process that enables organizations to turn their own documents, datasets, rules, workflows, and domain knowledge into specialized AI services.

---

## 1. Background and Problem Statement

Artificial intelligence is rapidly becoming a core capability for education, healthcare, public administration, and business operations. However, many organizations face a major adoption gap. They may understand that AI is important, but they often lack the practical knowledge, technical staff, clean data, governance process, or deployment pathway needed to apply AI safely and effectively.

Many available AI tools are designed either for general AI usage or for technically mature organizations. On one side, learning platforms teach students about AI and programming. On the other side, enterprise AI platforms provide advanced model training, data preparation, deployment, and governance functions. Between these two categories, there is a gap for non-technical organizations that need both education and implementation support.

The core problem is not only that organizations lack tools. The deeper problem is that they lack the capability to understand how AI systems are built, how data affects results, how trained systems should be evaluated, and how AI can be adapted to new problems.

This project addresses that gap by proposing an education-based no-code AI training platform that allows users to learn practical AI mechanics while creating specialized AI systems from their own data.

---

## 2. Project Vision

The vision is to create a platform that helps non-technical users move from AI literacy to AI implementation.

The project does not position AI as a black-box tool that users simply consume. Instead, it treats AI adoption as a capability-building process. Users should understand enough about AI to imagine new use cases, prepare suitable data, select the correct training approach, evaluate system quality, manage risk, and deploy AI responsibly.

### Core Vision Statement

**To build an education-based no-code AI training platform that enables non-technical organizations to understand, train, validate, and deploy specialized AI systems from their own data.**

### Core Mission

The mission is to reduce the barrier between AI knowledge and real AI implementation by combining structured learning, guided no-code training, evaluation tools, and flexible deployment pathways.

## 3. Proposed Solution

The proposed platform combines two main layers:

1. **AI Education Layer** — teaches practical AI mechanics in a simple, applied, non-mathematical way.
2. **No-Code AI Training Layer** — guides users through data preparation, AI customization, validation, deployment, and improvement.

The platform is not only a course platform and not only a no-code tool. It is a learning-to-building environment.

### 3.1 AI Education Layer

The education layer explains how AI works in a way that non-technical users can apply. It does not require advanced programming, mathematics, or machine learning theory. Instead, it focuses on practical understanding.

Key learning areas include:

- What AI can and cannot do
- How data quality affects AI quality
- Difference between base models, specialized AI, RAG, fine-tuning, and task-specific models
- How prompts and instructions shape model behavior
- How AI retrieves information from documents
- How classification, extraction, prediction, and forecasting work
- How hallucination happens and how to reduce it
- How to evaluate AI accuracy and safety
- How privacy and sensitive data should be handled
- How AI can be deployed through chat, API, website widgets, applications, and AI agents

The goal is to help users develop AI imagination and adaptability. Once users understand the practical mechanics, they can identify new problems where AI can be applied.

### 3.2 No-Code AI Training Layer

The no-code training layer allows users to apply what they learn immediately.

Users can:

- Upload documents, spreadsheets, websites, forms, images, or structured data
- Clean and organize data
- Detect duplicate or low-quality data
- Flag sensitive information
- Label examples and categories
- Select use-case templates
- Configure AI behavior
- Train or customize specialized AI systems
- Test with evaluation questions
- Review and approve outputs
- Deploy through chat, API, agent, or system integration
- Improve the AI through feedback and retraining

Training itself is designed to be no-code. However, deployment can be flexible. Simple deployments may use chat interfaces or copy-paste widgets, while deeper integration with hospital systems, government portals, internal databases, or enterprise applications may require APIs and developer support.

## 4. Core Framework

The proposed framework is:

## Learn → Prepare → Train → Validate → Deploy → Improve

### 4.1 Learn

Users first learn the practical mechanics behind the AI use case they want to build.

For example, before creating a document-based assistant, users learn what retrieval-augmented generation is, why document structure matters, and why the AI should answer only from approved sources.

### 4.2 Prepare

Users prepare their organization’s data. This may include uploading documents, cleaning spreadsheets, removing duplicates, redacting sensitive data, and organizing content by topic.

### 4.3 Train

Users train or customize the AI through guided no-code workflows. Depending on the use case, this may involve RAG, classification, extraction, forecasting, or fine-tuning-style behavior customization.

### 4.4 Validate

Users test the AI before deployment. Validation includes accuracy checks, hallucination checks, source-grounding tests, privacy checks, cost estimation, and human review.

### 4.5 Deploy

The AI can be deployed through chat interfaces, internal dashboards, website widgets, APIs, AI agents, or developer-assisted integrations.

### 4.6 Improve

The AI improves through feedback, correction, version control, monitoring, and retraining.

## 6. Differentiation from Existing Platforms

### 6.1 Difference from AIThaiGen

AIThaiGen is an AI and programming education platform. Its public website positions it as a platform for learning programming and AI through visual coding, flow-based coding, block programming, AI lessons, and real projects. It also highlights student smart-farming projects and states that more than 3,000 students have started learning with the platform.

AIThaiGen’s main output is learner development: students and beginners understand coding and AI concepts and can build learning projects.

This proposed project has a different output. It is designed to help users understand AI mechanics and then train organization-specific AI systems from real organizational data.

**Simple distinction:**

- AIThaiGen trains people to learn coding and AI.
- This project trains people to understand AI systems and then train specialized AI for organizational use.

### 6.2 Difference from Google Vertex AI AutoML

Google Vertex AI AutoML enables users to train machine learning models with minimal effort and machine learning expertise. Its workflow focuses on training and using models on Google Cloud.

The proposed project is not primarily a cloud ML service. It is an education-based adoption platform. Its value is not only automating training, but also teaching users what they are doing, why each step matters, and how to safely adapt AI to new organizational use cases.

### 6.3 Difference from AWS SageMaker Canvas

AWS SageMaker Canvas is a no-code ML interface that allows business analysts to create machine learning models without ML experience. It supports data preparation, model building, evaluation, deployment, and prediction generation.

The proposed project differs by focusing on users who may not yet be business analysts or cloud users. It starts from AI literacy and practical mechanics, then guides users into no-code training and deployment. It is education-first, not only tool-first.

### 6.4 Difference from Microsoft AI Builder

Microsoft AI Builder allows users to configure, train, publish, and use AI models inside Microsoft Power Platform. It is especially useful for organizations already using Power Apps, Power Automate, and Microsoft Dataverse.

The proposed project is platform-neutral. It is not limited to one productivity ecosystem. It is designed as a learning-to-building platform that can support different deployment surfaces, including chat, APIs, web widgets, AI agents, and developer-assisted integrations.

### 6.5 Difference from IBM watsonx.ai

IBM watsonx.ai and related enterprise AI approaches support prompt engineering, RAG, fine-tuning, and enterprise AI workflows. IBM explains that RAG, fine-tuning, and prompt engineering are optimization methods enterprises use to get more value from large language models, depending on use case and resources.

The proposed project makes these concepts understandable and usable for non-technical organizations. It does not compete directly with enterprise AI studios at the infrastructure level. Instead, it addresses the education and adoption gap before and during implementation.

### 6.6 Difference from Private and On-Premise AI Platforms

Private and on-premise AI platforms are important for regulated sectors such as healthcare, government, finance, and defense where data control is critical. However, these platforms are usually infrastructure-heavy and require technical teams.

The proposed project focuses on capability building first. It teaches users how to prepare data, understand risk, validate outputs, and manage AI responsibly. For sensitive organizations, deployment can later support private cloud or on-premise integration.

The unique value proposition is:

**An education-based no-code AI training platform that helps non-technical organizations understand AI mechanics, prepare their own data, train specialized AI systems, validate performance and safety, and deploy AI into real use cases.**

The platform’s uniqueness comes from combining:

## 8. Technical Concept

The technical architecture can be organized into seven layers.

### 8.1 Data Layer

Supports ingestion of:

- PDF documents
- Word documents
- Spreadsheets
- Google Sheets
- Websites
- FAQs
- Forms
- Images
- Database exports
- Chat or support logs

### 8.2 Data Preparation Layer

Includes:

- Data cleaning
- Duplicate detection
- Topic tagging
- Document splitting
- Sensitive data detection
- Redaction support
- Data quality scoring
- Dataset versioning

### 8.3 Training and Customization Layer

Supports:

- RAG-based knowledge assistants
- Classification models
- Extraction models
- Forecasting models
- Prompt behavior configuration
- Fine-tuning-style example learning
- Human-reviewed correction datasets

### 8.4 Model Layer

Can connect to multiple model providers, such as:

- OpenAI models
- Claude models
- Gemini models
- Open-source models
- Local/private models
- Future domain-specific or local-language models

The platform should remain model-neutral where possible to reduce vendor lock-in.

### 8.5 Evaluation Layer

Includes:

- Test question sets
- Accuracy scoring
- Source-grounding checks
- Hallucination detection
- Cost estimation
- Human review workflow
- Risk-level assessment
- Before/after comparison

### 8.6 Deployment Layer

Supports:

- Internal chat interface
- Website widget
- API endpoint
- AI agent interface
- Dashboard integration
- LINE, Teams, Slack, or other messaging channels
- Developer-assisted integration with existing systems

### 8.7 Governance and Improvement Layer

Includes:

- User permissions
- Audit logs
- Model/data versioning
- Approval workflows
- Feedback collection
- Retraining workflow
- Usage analytics
- Privacy controls

## 9. Research and Development Questions

A university-funded project can investigate several research questions:

1. How can practical AI mechanics be taught to non-technical users in a way that improves their ability to design AI use cases?
2. How can no-code interfaces reduce the barrier to organizational AI training?
3. What level of conceptual AI understanding is required before users can safely prepare data and validate AI outputs?
4. How can organizations evaluate hallucination, accuracy, privacy risk, and cost without technical expertise?
5. How can a learning-to-building platform improve AI adoption in schools, hospitals, public agencies, and SMEs?
6. What deployment models are most suitable for organizations with different levels of technical maturity?
7. How can human feedback and correction loops improve specialized AI over time?

## 10. Minimum Viable Product Scope

The MVP should avoid trying to support every AI use case immediately. A practical first version should focus on three high-value training modes.

### 10.1 Document Knowledge AI

Users upload organizational documents and create a knowledge assistant that answers based on approved sources.

Example use cases:

- School policy assistant
- Government service FAQ assistant
- Hospital SOP assistant
- SME internal knowledge assistant

### 10.2 Spreadsheet Insight and Prediction AI

Users upload structured data and create simple summaries, classifications, or forecasts.

Example use cases:

- Stock demand forecasting
- Queue demand prediction
- Sales summary assistant
- Student attendance risk analysis

### 10.3 Form and Document Extraction AI

Users upload forms or document examples and train the AI to extract structured fields.

Example use cases:

- Government application forms
- Hospital admin forms
- School registration documents
- SME invoices and order forms

## 11. Expected Impact

### 11.1 Educational Impact

The platform helps users move beyond passive AI usage. They learn how AI systems work and how to apply AI safely and creatively.

### 11.2 Organizational Impact

Organizations can convert internal knowledge and data into usable AI systems. This can reduce repetitive work, improve service response, support staff training, and help teams make better use of existing information.

### 11.3 Public-Sector Impact

Government agencies and public institutions can improve citizen services, document access, internal knowledge management, and administrative efficiency.

### 11.4 Healthcare Impact

Hospitals and clinics can improve non-diagnostic administrative support, staff onboarding, SOP access, and operational workflows while maintaining attention to privacy and governance.

### 11.5 Economic Impact

The platform can help SMEs and local organizations adopt AI without hiring full AI teams, reducing long-term cost and increasing digital competitiveness.

## 12. Risks and Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Users misunderstand AI limitations | Built-in education modules and warnings |
| Poor data quality creates poor AI results | Data readiness score and cleaning workflow |
| Hallucination or unsupported answers | Source-grounding, test sets, and human approval |
| Privacy leakage | Sensitive data detection, redaction, permissions, audit logs |
| Overpromising no-code deployment | Clearly separate no-code training from low-code/API deployment |
| Vendor lock-in | Model-neutral architecture |
| Unsafe healthcare use | Limit initial healthcare use cases to admin/SOP/non-diagnostic support |
| Low adoption by organizations | Use templates, workshops, and guided onboarding |

- Define education curriculum

### Phase 2: MVP Platform

## 14. Evaluation Metrics

The project should measure both learning outcomes and implementation outcomes.

### Learning Metrics

- AI concept understanding before and after training
- User confidence in designing AI use cases
- Ability to identify suitable data for AI
- Ability to explain AI limitations and risks
- Ability to select the correct AI training mode

### Platform Metrics

- Time from data upload to first working AI prototype
- Number of successful AI use cases created
- Data quality improvement score
- AI accuracy or answer-grounding score
- Human approval rate
- User correction frequency
- Cost per answer or task

### Organizational Metrics

- Reduction in repetitive staff questions
- Faster access to internal knowledge
- Reduced manual document-processing time
- Improved service response time
- User satisfaction
- Long-term adoption rate

## 15. Conclusion

This project proposes a new category between AI education and enterprise AI tooling: an education-based no-code AI training platform for organizational capability building.

AIThaiGen and similar platforms help learners understand coding and AI. Enterprise platforms such as Google Vertex AI AutoML, AWS SageMaker Canvas, Microsoft AI Builder, IBM watsonx.ai, and private AI systems provide powerful model-building and deployment tools. However, many organizations still lack the practical understanding and guided process needed to move from AI interest to safe, useful AI implementation.

This project fills that gap by combining practical AI education with no-code AI training, validation, governance, and deployment. Its goal is not only to help users build AI systems, but to help organizations become capable of adapting AI to new problems independently.

The proposed platform can support schools, hospitals, government agencies, universities, and SMEs by helping them transform their own knowledge and data into specialized AI systems while developing long-term AI capability.
